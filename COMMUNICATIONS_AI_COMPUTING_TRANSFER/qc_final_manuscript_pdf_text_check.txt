11   Abstract

12   Single-cell perturbation datasets often contain subset-confined responses rather than uniform mean shifts,
13   making target prioritization diﬀicult for average-based analyses. We present PGAA, a distribution-aware
14   computational framework for ranking heterogeneous transcriptional responses in single-cell perturbation
15   data. PGAA combines PGAA-W Wasserstein, a 99-point quantile-grid statistic for full-expression-
16   distribution shifts, with the S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H), a secondary statistic for responder-
17   associated expression-shape changes. Both statistics use permutation calibration and Storey upper-tail
18   diagnostics. In an Adamson 2016 unfolded-protein-response CRISPRi proof-of-principle benchmark,
19   PGAA-W Wasserstein achieved mean AUROC 0.786 across five pre-specified perturbations and mean
20   AUPRC 0.0191 against a 0.0065 random baseline. A Norman 2019 multi-perturbation CRISPRa extension
21   supported PGAA-W as the default ranking statistic in curated target-panel checks, while a focused Norman
22   CEBPE example illustrated the narrower role of the S2 statistic. A processed-source-data MMD-PSM
23   comparison and observational single-cell datasets are retained as bounded comparator and marker-recovery
24   stress checks.
25   Keywords: single-cell RNA-seq, Perturb-seq, computational biology, Wasserstein distance, histogram-shape
26   statistic, distributional ranking, CRISPR screening


27   1. Introduction

28   Single-cell perturbation screens 1–4 provide a scalable way to connect genetic or transcriptional perturba-
29   tions to cellular phenotypes. A central computational task in these datasets is to rank genes or response
30   programs whose expression differs between perturbed and control cells. The standard analysis compares
31   mean expression of each non-target gene between groups. This works well when a perturbation produces a
32   uniform transcriptional shift: knock out a transcription factor, its targets drop, and a t-test, Wilcoxon test,
33   MAST, or SCEPTRE can detect the difference 5,6 . Together with preprocessing workflows in Seurat 7,8 and
34   Scanpy 9 , these methods form the standard Perturb-seq analysis toolkit.
35   Many perturbation responses, however, are not well summarized by a mean shift. CRISPRa 10 can activate a
36   target in only a fraction of cells. A weak perturbation may shift variance, create responder and non-responder
37   subpopulations, or change the shape of an expression distribution while leaving the mean only modestly
38   changed. Similar response structures can also appear in marker-anchored contrasts from patient-derived
39   single-cell data, where disease-associated programs often mark subsets of malignant, immune, stromal, or
40   inflammatory cells rather than an entire population. A method that only asks whether average expression
41   changed can under-rank such signals and can make downstream prioritization less interpretable.
42   Few existing Perturb-seq methods make distribution-shape changes a primary output, although
43   heterogeneity-aware approaches are emerging 11 . This leaves a gap between calibrated mean-shift
44   testing and the practical need to rank heterogeneous responses for follow-up experiments. The gap is


                                                           1
45   computational rather than only biological: users need statistics that compare full expression distributions,
46   calibration diagnostics that reveal when a ranking is trustworthy, and software that can be applied
47   consistently across perturbation screens and marker-anchored single-cell contrasts.
48   We present PGAA, a distribution-aware framework and software implementation for heterogeneous single-
49   cell response prioritization. PGAA uses two complementary statistics sharing a common calibration infras-
50   tructure: the PGAA-W Wasserstein quantile-grid statistic captures one-dimensional distributional shifts,
51   and the S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H) ranks expression-shape changes consistent with responder-
52   associated heterogeneity. The mathematical target is to rank non-target genes by perturbation-associated
53   distributional discrepancy after coarse cell-state and library-size adjustment. The novelty is not the in-
54   vention of Wasserstein distance or histogram peak scoring; it is their calibration-aware adaptation into a
55   reproducible single-cell perturbation ranking layer with explicit evidence boundaries. Additional modules for
56   conditional MI and Fisher NB scoring are provided as software extensions. All statistics are accompanied by
57   Storey upper-tail calibration diagnostics that let users assess reliability on their own data before interpreting
58   results. Figure 1a illustrates the response regimes motivating PGAA, and Figure 1b summarizes the analysis
59   workflow.
60   We evaluate PGAA across experimental Perturb-seq benchmarks, simulation ablations, distribution-aware
61   comparators, and external marker-recovery stress checks. Adamson 2016 UPR CRISPRi and Norman 2019
62   CRISPRa provide real perturbation benchmarks with complementary response regimes. The Norman anal-
63   yses include a multi-perturbation PGAA-W extension across KLF1, CEBPE, and CEBPA with KS as an
64   additional distribution-aware baseline, followed by a focused CEBPE S2 example. Simulations define the
65   operating regimes of the Wasserstein and S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H). A processed-source-data
66   MMD-PSM comparison is included as an additional distribution-aware baseline for the Norman CEBPE
67   contrast. Observational scRNA-seq datasets in chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), sepsis, rheumatoid
68   arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease, and PBMCs are used only to ask whether distributional ranking can
69   recover known marker biology in medically relevant single-cell contexts. These analyses support marker

73   This separation is used throughout the manuscript: perturbation benchmarks assess ranking behavior, ob-
74   servational datasets are used only as marker-recovery stress checks, and calibration analyses define when a
75   score should be interpreted. With these evidence boundaries stated, Figure 1 gives the conceptual overview
76   of the response regimes and the PGAA workflow.
77   The statistics in PGAA are deliberately assigned different roles. PGAA-W Wasserstein measures loca-
78   tion, spread and other one-dimensional distributional shifts and is the default score. KS measures the
79   maximum empirical CDF discrepancy and is used as a distribution-aware comparator. MMD-PSM and re-
80   lated energy or kernel methods test broader distributional discrepancies but can be computationally heavier
81   and depend on implementation choices. S2 histogram-shape measures peak-structure changes, including
82   responder-associated expression-shape shifts, and is treated as a secondary diagnostic. Mean-based tests
83   remain useful when the response is dominated by a location shift but can under-rank subset-confined or
84   shape-changing responses.

86    2.1 Framework overview and evidence boundaries

87    PGAA is designed as a modular computational ranking layer for heterogeneous single-cell responses. The
88    Wasserstein statistic is the primary starting score for full-distribution shifts, whereas the S2 histogram-shape
89    statistic (PGAA-H) is a secondary diagnostic for responder-associated expression-shape changes. The frame-
90    work first adjusts expression for coarse cell-state and library-size effects, then ranks genes by distributional
91    discrepancy between perturbed and control cells. Within-cluster permutation is used to provide empirical
92    ranking evidence, and the Storey upper-tail diagnostic is used as a guardrail for calibration rather than as
93    a guarantee of genome-wide discovery.
94    Figure 1 summarizes the response-regime problem and the analysis workflow. The figure intentionally sepa-
95    rates the default PGAA-W score from the secondary S2 statistic, because the benchmarks below support a
96    hierarchy rather than two interchangeable discovery tests.


97    2.2 Independent Perturb-seq benchmark in unfolded-protein-response biology

98    To test PGAA on a small independent Perturb-seq dataset with a complementary perturbation modality
99    and a disease-relevant stress-response pathway, we applied both statistics to the Adamson et al. (2016) UPR
100   CRISPRi screen (GSE90546). After QC, 5,680 K562 cells were retained across five well-characterized sgRNA
101   perturbations targeting UPR genes (SPI1, ZNF326, BHLHE40, CREB1, DDIT3; 468-686 cells each) and a
102   non-targeting control (1,759 cells). The reference positive set was a curated UPR marker set covering IRE1,
103   PERK, ATF6, ERAD, and chaperone branches, of which 13 markers were present in the 2,000-gene HVG
104   subset (Supplementary Table 1).
105   Across the five perturbations, the Wasserstein statistic achieved a mean AUROC of 0.786 (range 0.767-0.806,
106   mean AUPRC 0.0191) for recovering known UPR genes (Table 2). By comparison, Wilcoxon, t-test, and
107   MAST achieved mean AUROCs of 0.529, 0.523, and 0.406 respectively in the same benchmark. The S2
108   histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H) achieved a mean AUROC of 0.748 (range 0.658-0.833, mean AUPRC
109   0.0253). For the BHLHE40 knockdown, this histogram-shape statistic had the highest AUROC and AUPRC
110   among the compared methods (AUROC 0.833, AUPRC 0.0594 vs. Wasserstein AUROC 0.788), consistent
111   with a heterogeneous responder-associated expression pattern (Supplementary Figure 1). Because only
112   13 of the 2,000 HVGs were UPR positives, the random AUPRC baseline was 0.0065; observed AUPRC
113   values correspond to 2.9x and 3.9x enrichment over random expectation for the Wasserstein and histogram-
114   shape statistics, respectively. These perturbations were selected a priori based on UPR annotation in the
115   original study and a minimum cell-count threshold of at least 400 cells, not based on PGAA performance
116   (Supplementary Table 2). The Adamson benchmark supports proof-of-principle prioritization in a small
117   curated UPR setting, but it does not establish general performance across perturbation modalities, cell
118   types, or disease models.

120   mark design, average AUROC, AUPRC relative to the random positive-rate baseline, and perturbation-level

126   2.3 A Norman multi-perturbation extension supports PGAA-W as the default ranking statistic

127   To broaden the real Perturb-seq evaluation beyond the small Adamson UPR benchmark, we added a Norman
128   2019 multi-perturbation CRISPRa extension covering KLF1, CEBPE, and CEBPA. Each perturbation was
129   compared with non-targeting controls using 2,000 HVGs plus forced inclusion of the pre-specified target-
130   panel genes. This forced-inclusion step is important: none of the KLF1, CEBPE, or CEBPA positive-panel
131   genes were marked as HVGs in the processed Norman h5ad file, so a strict HVG-only benchmark would have
132   silently removed the evaluation genes. The script reports a panel audit, gene-level scores, and a metadata
133   table in the supplementary software archive.
134   Across the three perturbations, PGAA-W Wasserstein produced higher target-panel AUROC than the KS
135   statistic, Welch t statistic, or Wilcoxon rank-sum score in this curated-panel benchmark (Supplementary
136   Table 3). The effect was not a claim of high-precision discovery: only 1-2 positive genes appeared in the top


                                                           6
137   100 for each PGAA-W run, and AUPRC values must be read against random baselines of 0.0045-0.0079.
138   The result is therefore useful as a broader perturbation-level ranking check for PGAA-W, not as a substitute
139   for genome-scale perturbation validation or original-study DEG benchmarking.


140   2.4 The S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H) provides a narrow CEBPE CRISPRa ranking
141   example

142   Norman et al. (2019) used CRISPRa to activate CEBPE in a K562 Perturb-seq screen 3 ; K562 is a human
143   leukemia cell line 12 . CEBPE is a myeloid transcription factor whose known targets include nine neutrophil
144   granule proteins: ELANE, AZU1, MPO, LYZ, CTSG, GFI1, PRTN3, DEFA1, and RNASE2 13,14 . This
145   benchmark is not a clinical disease cohort, but it is relevant to hematologic differentiation biology and tests
146   the response regime that motivated PGAA: a regulator is activated, but the downstream expression response
147   may be heterogeneous across cells.
148   SCEPTRE and the Wasserstein statistic rank this target poorly. SCEPTRE places ELANE at rank
149   1761/2012 (p = 0.92). The Wasserstein statistic places it at rank 1452/2012 (p = 0.223). Neither method
150   ranks ELANE among its strongest CEBPE candidates in this analysis. As an additional distribution-aware
151   comparator, an MMD-PSM analysis on the same processed CEBPE contrast achieved target-set AUROC
152   0.499 and 0/9 nominal CEBPE target hits. This comparator is useful because it tests a distributional null,
153   but it did not prioritize the curated CEBPE target program in this processed-source-data benchmark.
154   The S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H) (n_bins = 20, 500 permutations) ranks ELANE at position 57
155   (p = 0.04), a 25-fold rank improvement over the Wasserstein statistic (Table 3). With 500 permutations,
156   this p-value is interpreted as ranking evidence rather than genome-wide significance, and no FDR-controlled
157   CEBPE gene discovery is claimed. The p-value distribution is acceptable in this pre-specified setting (un-
158   capped Storey upper-tail ratio = 1.32 with 200 permutations in the sensitivity run; approximately 1.0 with
159   500 permutations). The top genes by raw S2 value (SLC45A1, DLX2, ATP5E) are not known CEBPE
160   targets; ELANE’s stronger rank by permutation p-value reflects its favorable null distribution relative to
161   other genes. Across the full nine-gene CEBPE target set, only ELANE reaches p < 0.05 in the S2 analysis;
162   PRTN3 is also within the top 100 by permutation p-value but does not pass the nominal threshold (p =
163   0.068). The AUROC is 0.476 and AUPRC is 0.0076. Thus, the Norman result should be read as a focused
164   ELANE/PRTN3 ranking signal, not broad recovery of the complete granulocytic target program. As a
165   calibration check, we applied the S2 statistic to all six perturbations using the CEBPE target gene set as
166   the reference positive set. ELANE was ranked strongly for CEBPE and also for the severely over-sensitive
167   BAK1 perturbation, where the tail ratio was 0.10. For the well-calibrated KLF1 perturbation, ELANE was
168   not supported (p = 0.70, uncapped tail ratio = 1.15). For CBL, SLC4A1, and DUSP9, ELANE p-values
169   were 0.58, 0.16, and 0.90 respectively. This analysis is treated as perturbation-level calibration context
170   rather than specificity proof; over-sensitive perturbations should not be interpreted as formal discoveries.
171   Target-level details are provided in Supplementary Table 4.

179   2.5 Perturbation-specific calibration defines guardrails for S2 ranking

180   Applying the S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H) to six different perturbations from the Norman 2019
181   screen reveals that calibration is far from consistent. The uncapped Storey upper-tail ratio ranges from
182   1.15 for KLF1, a well-calibrated erythroid transcription-factor perturbation, to 0.10 for BAK1, a severely


                                                          8
183   over-sensitive perturbation. Intermediate cases include CBL (tail ratio = 0.72), SLC4A1 (0.67), DUSP9
184   (0.68), and CEBPE in the n_bins = 50 calibration run (0.25). Figure 4 shows the full calibration pattern.
185   KLF1 drives a clean erythroid program. CEBPE CRISPRa and BAK1 do not. The calibration spread
186   reflects this biology, and the practical takeaway is simple: include a negative control perturbation and
187   check the upper-tail calibration diagnostic before interpreting S2 ranks. Importantly, the CEBPE tail ratio
188   of 0.25 reported here comes from a calibration run with n_bins = 50 and n_perms = 200; the main
189   analysis in Section 2.4 uses n_bins = 20 and n_perms = 500, which yields an upper-tail ratio approximately
190   1.0-1.3 before capping. The corresponding QQ plot and permutation p-value histogram are provided in
191   Supplementary Figure 3. The discrepancy illustrates how strongly calibration depends on both bin count
192   and permutation depth, reinforcing the need for the pilot sweep recommended in Section 2.6.

193   2.6 Sensitivity to histogram bin count

194   The S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H) depends on the number of histogram bins, and the dependence
195   is non-trivial. We tested n_bins values of 20, 30, 50, 75, 100 and 150 on Norman 2019 CEBPE, using
196   200 permutations for the sensitivity sweep; the main CEBPE result uses 500 permutations. The full sweep
197   is reported in Supplementary Table 5. In brief, ELANE ranks well at n_bins = 20 (rank 32, p = 0.025,
198   uncapped tail ratio = 1.32 in the 200-permutation sweep), collapses at n_bins = 30 (rank 1807, p=0.672), and
199   larger bin counts produce many nominally significant genes with low tail-ratio values (0.23-0.25). This is a
200   sharp failure mode rather than a gradual degradation. We therefore treat S2 statistic as a guarded diagnostic
201   with a pre-specified decision rule: choose n_bins from a small pilot grid before interpreting the perturbation
202   of interest, require an acceptable upper-tail diagnostic on a negative-control or unrelated perturbation, and
203   do not tune n_bins to improve a target-gene rank. We recommend n_bins = 20 as the manuscript starting
204   setting, with a pilot sweep over n_bins = 10, 20, 30 and 50 on a control or null perturbation to verify that
205   the chosen value does not fall into a peak-merging failure mode.


206   2.7 Simulation ablation maps each statistic to its regime

207   To systematically assess when each statistic performs best, we simulated three perturbation types at seven
208   effect sizes from theta = 0.1 to theta = 1.0, with three replicates each (63 conditions total). These simulations
209   are intended as operating-regime diagnostics rather than a full generative model of scRNA-seq count data.
210   Type A (pure mean shift) is the Wasserstein statistic’s home territory (TPR = 0.97 at theta = 1.0). Type
211   C (mean + bimodality) favors the Wasserstein statistic as well. The most informative condition is Type B,
212   where only 40% of perturbed cells respond. Here the Wasserstein statistic and the exploratory combined
213   z-score are competitive (TPR = 0.52-0.85 depending on theta), while S2 reaches only TPR = 0.12. This
214   simulation uses partial bimodality–the two modes overlap in the 40%-response regime. The Norman 2019


                                                             9
215   CEBPE analysis therefore should not be read as broad evidence that S2 prioritizes all heterogeneous re-
216   sponses; rather, it illustrates a narrower ranking use case in which a responder-associated expression pattern
217   can improve the rank of selected targets.
218   These results support a simple analysis hierarchy. The Wasserstein statistic is the starting ranking score
219   for standard knockout or overexpression screens and for unknown response modes. The S2 histogram-
220   shape statistic (PGAA-H) should be used only as a secondary diagnostic when discrete responder states are
221   biologically plausible, and only after negative-control calibration and bin-sensitivity checks. The combined
222   z-score can be used as an exploratory ranking score when both mean/distributional shift and shape change
223   are expected, but it is not treated as an inferential discovery test here.
224   The full simulation plot is provided in Supplementary Figure 4.


225   2.8 Observational marker-recovery stress checks remain secondary evidence

226   As an external stress check outside curated perturbation benchmarks, Wasserstein enriches known pathway
227   markers 2.1-5.8x over background across five observational single-cell datasets with clinical or disease rele-
228   vance (CLL 4.0x, Sepsis 2.1x, RA 2.5x, PBMC 2.9x, IBD 5.8x). On CLL, the B-cell receptor signaling gene
229   set (CD79A, CD79B, MS4A1, CD24, BANK1, LYN, BLNK, SYK, BTK, PLCG2, PIK3CD, CD19, CD22)
230   appears prominently in the top 100, with AUROC 0.87 for known-marker recovery (Supplementary Table 6).
231   This result is biologically coherent because B-cell receptor signaling is a central axis in CLL, but the analysis
232   should be read as marker prioritization from public observational data rather than as causal or biomarker
233   evidence.
234   These five datasets are observational–the “perturbation” is defined by binning cells on endogenous marker
235   or pathway-associated expression, not by an experimental intervention–so the results demonstrate marker
236   recovery rather than causal interpretation. In the observational CLL marker-recovery setting, Wasserstein
237   produced biologically coherent rankings but was not uniformly superior to all conventional ranking baselines.
238   Its value here is as a distribution-aware prioritization score for heterogeneous disease-state contrasts, not as
239   a replacement for standard marker-ranking methods.
240   The CLL TCL1A case shows how the two distributional statistics can prioritize different marker programs.
241   Among the top 100 genes ranked by each statistic, only six overlap. The Wasserstein statistic enriches B-cell
242   receptor markers (CD79A rank 9, CD79B rank 48, MS4A1 rank 53), matching the malignant B-cell biology
243   of CLL. The S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H) picks up T-cell receptor genes (TRBV7-6, TRAV12-1,
244   CD3E, CD3D) that Wasserstein misses entirely, consistent with a distinct immune-context signal rather than
245   the same B-cell receptor program. The exploratory combined z-score recovers 3 BCR and 11 TCR genes at
246   nominal p < 0.05, versus 4+3 for Wasserstein alone and 0+7 for S2 alone; these nominal combined scores
247   are used only to illustrate rank complementarity.
248   The CLL analysis uses rank-based inverse-normal scores (Supplementary Methods, CLL rank-score analysis)
249   rather than full permutation calibration–a lighter-weight approach appropriate when only relative gene
250   ordering matters rather than formal significance testing (Supplementary Figure 5). The cross-dataset marker-
251   recovery source data and CLL comparator results are retained in Supplementary Table 6. We do not use
252   these observational datasets as causal or clinical evidence. Figure 5 summarizes the external marker-recovery
253   stress checks and the CLL comparator analysis.

254   3. Discussion
255   We set out to assess whether distribution-aware statistics could improve computational prioritization of
256   single-cell perturbation programs when the signal is heterogeneous rather than a simple mean shift. The
257   answer, based on Perturb-seq benchmarks, simulation ablations, distribution-aware comparators, and obser-
258   vational marker-recovery stress checks, is cautiously positive. On the small Adamson 2016 UPR CRISPRi
259   benchmark, the Wasserstein statistic achieved a mean AUROC of 0.786 across five perturbations, higher than
260   Wilcoxon (0.529), t-test (0.523), and MAST (0.406) in this benchmark. Because the positive set contains
261   only 13 genes among 2,000 HVGs, AUPRC values must be interpreted against the 0.0065 random baseline
262   rather than as large absolute precision values. In the Norman 2019 multi-perturbation extension, PGAA-
263   W Wasserstein had higher target-panel AUROC than KS, Welch t, and Wilcoxon for KLF1, CEBPE, and
264   CEBPA, but top-100 positive recovery remained sparse. In the BHLHE40 knockdown case, the S2 histogram-
265   shape statistic (PGAA-H) had the highest AUROC among the compared methods (0.833), consistent with
266   a heterogeneous responder-associated expression pattern. On Norman 2019 CEBPE CRISPRa, S2 ranked
267   the known neutrophil granule target ELANE at position 57, while SCEPTRE and the Wasserstein statistic
268   placed it at 1761 and 1452; across the full nine-gene target set, however, recovery remained limited. The
269   MMD-PSM distribution-aware baseline achieved AUROC 0.499 with 0/9 nominal target hits in the same
270   processed CEBPE comparison. Across five observational datasets used as external stress checks, Wasserstein
271   ranking recovered known marker programs, including B-cell receptor signaling in CLL.
272   These results suggest a practical two-pronged strategy for distribution-aware single-cell perturbation analysis.
273   The Wasserstein statistic is a useful starting distributional score when the perturbation type is unknown,
274   including when marker-anchored scRNA-seq contrasts are used to prioritize candidate response programs for
275   follow-up. The S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H) fills a narrower niche–CRISPRa screens, weak-effect
276   perturbations, and any setting where the response is expected to be heterogeneous–but requires the user to
277   verify calibration via the Storey upper-tail diagnostic and to run a pilot n_bins sweep before committing
278   to results. PGAA-H’s strong dependence on histogram bin count (values at n_bins = 20 and n_bins =
279   50 correlate at only r = 0.12) means it should be treated as a ranking tool rather than a formal discovery
280   procedure unless substantially more permutations are used.
281   Several limitations should be noted. First, the Adamson 2016 benchmark is small and has a sparse positive
282   set, so it is proof-of-principle evidence rather than broad validation. Second, the Norman multi-perturbation
283   extension uses curated target-program panels rather than an original-study genome-wide DEG gold standard;
284   its sparse top-rank recovery should be viewed as ranking enrichment, not strong discovery performance.
285   Third, S2 is calibrated differently for different perturbations; the six-perturbation calibration check showed
286   uncapped upper-tail ratios ranging from 0.10 (BAK1, over-sensitive) to 1.15 (KLF1, well-calibrated), so a
287   negative control perturbation is essential. Fourth, the MI and Fisher NB modules are provided as software
288   extensions but have not been benchmarked. Fifth, our simulation uses Gaussian noise for computational
289   eﬀiciency; we verified that S2 does not spuriously increase under a zero-inflated negative-binomial null
290   (mean S2 = 0.031 with 30% dropout; Supplementary Methods, simulation and zero-inflation controls),
291   consistent with evidence that droplet-based scRNA-seq data are well-modeled by the negative binomial
292   without explicit zero-inflation 15 . Sixth, all primary benchmarks use K-means (k = 5) as a cell-state proxy;
293   sensitivity analyses over k = 3, 5, 10 and no residualization are recommended but not yet completed in this
294   submission package. Seventh, broader genome-scale evaluation against SCEPTRE-family and perturbation-
295   specific methods remains necessary. Finally, the observational disease datasets do not establish new causal
296   disease mechanisms or clinical biomarkers; instead, they show how PGAA can prioritize disease-relevant
297   heterogeneous transcriptional responses for experimental follow-up.
298   PGAA fits into the growing ecosystem of Perturb-seq analysis tools and addresses a complementary computa-
299   tional use case. The pertpy framework 11 provides an end-to-end pipeline into which PGAA’s statistics could
300   be integrated as modular ranking components. Mixscape 16 handles the complementary problem of identi-
301   fying cells with failed perturbations, while scMAGeCK 17 operates at the CRISPR screen level. SCEPTRE
      5
302     and its robust extension 18 set a high standard for calibrated permutation testing of mean shifts; PGAA
303   extends this paradigm to distributional and histogram-shape shifts that may matter when only a subset of
304   cells responds. Recent perturbation-response scoring methods quantify heterogeneous response strength at
305   the single-cell level, whereas PGAA operates at the non-target-gene ranking level after coarse cell-state and


                                                            12
306   library-size adjustment 19 . Large curated resources such as scPerturb make broader cross-dataset bench-
307   marking feasible, but this submission restricts itself to source-data-supported proof-of-principle and curated
308   target-panel checks 20 . Thus, PGAA should be read as a calibrated gene-level distributional ranking compo-
309   nent that can sit alongside perturbation-quality, single-cell response-strength, and formal association-testing
310   methods rather than as a replacement for them.
311   The most immediate priority is broader genome-scale evaluation on scPerturb-scale collections, including
312   Replogle 2020/2022 and additional perturbation screens, where thousands of perturbations would allow a
313   systematic assessment of calibration behavior and the identification of perturbation-level features that predict
314   whether Wasserstein or S2 will prove more informative. The main value of PGAA is not to replace mechanistic
315   validation, but to provide a transparent and reproducible ranking layer for heterogeneous responses that can
316   be followed up in perturbation screens, disease models, patient-derived systems, or therapeutic perturbation
317   experiments.


318   4. Methods
319   4.1 Setup and residualization

320   We work with expression matrices normalized to 10,000 counts per cell and transformed with log1p. After
321   standard QC and highly-variable gene selection (2,000 genes), each cell i has a perturbation label D_i in
322   {0,1} and covariates Z_i. Before applying any statistic, we remove cell-type and library-size effects by
323   ordinary least-squares residualization:
324   X_tilde = X_res = (I - H_Z) X
325   H_Z = Z (Z^T Z)^+ Z^T
326   where H_Z is the projection matrix for the covariate design matrix Z, X_tilde (also denoted X_res) is the
327   residualized expression matrix, and (Z^T Z)^+ denotes the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse used for numerical
328   stability. The matrix Z contains an intercept, k - 1 K-means cluster dummy variables (k = 5, one reference
329   cluster) 21 , and standardized library size. This step is applied to the PGAA-W and S2 statistics; the MI and
330   Fisher NB exploratory scores operate on the original normalized matrix. The value of k is fixed throughout
331   the benchmark as a coarse cell-state proxy rather than estimated from the data.
332   For a target gene g*, we score each non-target gene g for differences between perturbed and control cells in
333   mean, shape, or dependency structure.


334   4.2 Wasserstein distance

335   We approximate the one-dimensional Wasserstein distance by comparing empirical quantile functions on a
336   fixed 99-point percentile grid. Let Q_hat_{g,d}(u) denote the empirical u-quantile of residualized expression
337   for gene g among cells with perturbation indicator D_i = d. The score is
338   PGAA-W(g) = (1/99) sum_{r=1}^{99} |Q_hat_{g,1}(r/100) - Q_hat_{g,0}(r/100)|
339   Wasserstein distances provide a non-parametric way to compare full one-dimensional distributions and can
340   respond to changes in location, spread, or shape. This makes the statistic useful when the biological signal
341   is not limited to a mean shift. The 1D case has an eﬀicient quantile-based computation 22,23 .
342   We compute p-values via within-cluster permutation 24,25 . For B permutations, we shuffle D within each
343   K-means cluster, recompute the Wasserstein statistic, and use the plus-one estimator
344   p_perm = [1 + sum_{b=1}^{B} I{S_b >= S_obs}] / (B + 1)
345   where S_obs is the observed statistic and S_b is the statistic from permutation b. The cluster-stratified
346   shuffle preserves cell-type composition while randomizing the perturbation assignment.
347   A note on numerical consistency. The observed and permuted W1 statistics are computed with the same
348   99-quantile approximation. This is essential because permutation p-values compare the observed statistic


                                                            13
349   directly with its empirical null distribution. We verified calibration empirically: under the null, the observed
350   false positive rate is 0.057 (30 simulations, 95% CI [0.021, 0.079]).


351   4.3 S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H)

352   The S2 histogram-shape statistic (PGAA-H) summarizes peak-structure changes in the one-dimensional
353   expression histogram, such as the emergence or disappearance of peaks. For each gene g, we compute
354   histograms of the residualized expression vector for perturbed cells (D_i = 1) and control cells (D_i = 0)
355   using gene-specific shared bin edges for that same gene g. The manuscript starting setting is n_bins =
356   20 equally spaced bins over that gene’s pooled perturbed-control expression range. We then identify local
357   maxima and assign each peak a death level defined by the higher of the adjacent saddle levels, using the
358   nearest higher peak or boundary on each side. This is a one-dimensional peak-prominence construction
359   derived from the Elder Rule used in topological data analysis 26–29 . The peak-prominence value is the peak
360   height minus this death level. For each condition, we keep the three largest peak-prominence values and
361   zero-pad when fewer than three peaks are present.
362   The S2 statistic is the root-mean-square distance between the top-3 peak-prominence vectors:
363   PGAA-H(g) = sqrt((1/3) * sum_{r=1}^{3} (v_{g,1,r} - v_{g,0,r})^2)
364   Here v_{g,d,r} is the r-th largest peak-prominence value for gene g in condition d, where d = 1 denotes
365   perturbed cells and d = 0 denotes control cells. This plain-text notation is used to keep the formula stable
366   in DOCX and PDF conversion.
367   When a CRISPRa perturbation activates a gene in only a fraction of cells, the expression histogram can
368   change from a single dominant pattern to a heterogeneous responder-associated pattern. The dominant
369   peak-prominence value may then increase substantially, yielding large S2 values. A pure mean shift that
370   preserves the histogram shape leaves S2 comparatively unchanged. The contrast between these two scenarios
371   is what makes S2 complementary to the Wasserstein statistic, but S2 is interpreted here as a ranking and
372   diagnostic statistic rather than as a standalone genome-wide discovery test.
373   The choice of n_bins matters. Our sensitivity analysis (Section 2.6) tests n_bins values of 20, 30, 50, 75,
374   100, and 150. The manuscript starting setting, n_bins = 20, is acceptably calibrated in the Norman 2019
375   CEBPE run (uncapped upper-tail ratio approximately 1.0-1.3 depending on permutation depth). Larger
376   values can produce more nominal hits at the cost of over-sensitive calibration. For new analyses, we rec-
377   ommend a guarded S2 workflow: pre-specify a small n_bins grid such as 10, 20, 30 and 50; evaluate the
378   grid on a negative-control, null, or unrelated perturbation; keep a setting only if the upper-tail diagnostic is
379   acceptable and the p-value histogram is not over-sensitive; and then apply the chosen setting unchanged to
380   the perturbation of interest. We use 500 within-cluster permutations for the null. With 500 permutations,
381   the minimum attainable p-value is 1/501, approximately 0.0020, which is above the Bonferroni threshold for
382   2,000 genes (2.5e-5). The S2 permutation p-values therefore support ranking-based evaluation (AUROC)
383   rather than formal FDR-controlled discovery; for genome-wide significance, we recommend n_perms >=
384   40,000.


385   4.4 Additional modules

386   Two additional scoring modules are included in the software package as extensions. The conditional MI
387   module explores whether perturbation alters gene-gene dependency structure via k-NN entropy estimators
      30
388      . The Fisher NB module explores perturbation-induced shifts using the Fisher information metric on the
389   negative binomial family. These modules are not benchmarked or claimed as primary contributions; they
390   are provided for users who wish to explore dependency or count-model effects. Algorithm details are given
391   in the Supplementary Methods.




                                                            14
392   4.5 Combined exploratory z-score

393   Under independence and correct calibration, the combined exploratory z-score is
394   z_comb = (1/sqrt(k)) sum_{j=1}^{k} Phi^{-1}(1 - p_j).
395   This score is asymptotically standard normal under H0. We use k = 2 (Wasserstein + PGAA-H) as an
396   exploratory rank-combination score. Because both statistics are computed from the same expression dis-
397   tributions, the independence assumption is unlikely to hold exactly; formal combined-score inference would
398   require an empirical covariance adjustment, Brown’s method, or a permutation-calibrated combination. In
399   this manuscript, the combined score is used only for exploratory ranking. For Norman 2019 CEBPE, S2
400   alone ranked ELANE higher than the combination.


401   4.6 Calibration diagnostics

402   For calibration, PGAA reports the Storey upper-tail diagnostic 31,32 . Let p_i be the permutation p-value
403   for ranked gene i, let m be the number of ranked genes, and set lambda = 0.5. The uncapped upper-tail
404   calibration ratio is
405   R_lambda = #{i: p_i > lambda} / [(1 - lambda) * m].
406   The capped Storey null-fraction estimate is pi0_hat(lambda) = min(1, R_lambda). The uncapped ratio
407   R_lambda is reported separately as a calibration diagnostic: values near or above 1 indicate no obvious
408   excess of small p-values, whereas values far below 1 indicate over-sensitivity or anti-conservative behavior.
409   This wording avoids the mathematically incorrect implication that the null fraction pi0 itself can exceed 1.
410   We also recommend running the S2 statistic on an unrelated target gene from the same screen (e.g., KLF1
411   from Norman 2019) as a negative perturbation control, and verifying that the calibration diagnostic remains
412   acceptable.


413   4.7 Datasets

414   We use seven publicly available datasets: five observational scRNA-seq datasets for marker-recovery stress
415   checks and two Perturb-seq datasets for method benchmarking. The observational analyses include CLL
416   (GSE111014; 36,601 cells, subsampled to 20,000; TCL1A high/low grouping with B-cell receptor mark-
417   ers), sepsis (GSE167363; 64,244 cells, subsampled to 20,000; TCR pathway markers), rheumatoid arthri-
418   tis (GSE159117; 10,499 cells; cytokine pathway markers), the 10x Genomics 3k demo PBMC dataset 33
419   (2,700 cells; multi-lineage markers), and inflammatory bowel disease (GSE116222; 11,175 cells; gut epithelial
420   disease-associated markers).
421   The perturbation benchmarks are Norman 2019 (GSE133344) and Adamson 2016 (GSE90546). For Norman
422   2019, we use the K562 CRISPRa screen for the CEBPE ranking example, the six-perturbation S2 calibration
423   check, and the multi-perturbation PGAA-W extension across KLF1, CEBPE, and CEBPA. The six S2
424   calibration perturbations are CEBPE, KLF1, SLC4A1, BAK1, DUSP9, and CBL; the CEBPE target set is
425   ELANE, AZU1, MPO, LYZ, CTSG, GFI1, PRTN3, DEFA1, and RNASE2. The Norman multi-perturbation
426   extension uses curated target-program panels for KLF1, CEBPE, and CEBPA, 2,000 HVGs plus forced
427   inclusion of the panel genes, and non-targeting controls sampled at up to a 3:1 control-to-perturbed ratio.
428   The comparator scores in that extension are PGAA-W Wasserstein, two-sample KS statistic, absolute Welch t
429   statistic, and Wilcoxon -log10(p). For Adamson 2016, we analyze five pre-specified UPR-associated CRISPRi
430   perturbations (SPI1, ZNF326, BHLHE40, CREB1, and DDIT3) against non-targeting controls, using the
431   curated UPR marker set in Supplementary Table 1.
432   All datasets are normalized to 10,000 counts per cell and log-transformed after quality control. Analyses use
433   2,000 highly variable genes, with forced inclusion of target genes where needed.

434   4.8 Implementation

435   PGAA is implemented in Python and R. The Python package (pgaa/) depends on NumPy, SciPy, Pandas,
436   Scanpy 9 , and scikit-learn; the R package (pgaa_r/) uses only base R plus the MASS library. The submitted
437   package includes Python and R smoke/regression checks for the core Wasserstein and S2 calculations. On
438   a single CPU core, the Wasserstein statistic processes 2,000 genes with 2,000 permutations in about five
439   minutes; S2 with 500 permutations takes under two minutes. Preprocessing follows standard scRNA-seq
440   workflows 34,35 , and the end-to-end ranking workflow is summarized in Supplementary Figure 6.
