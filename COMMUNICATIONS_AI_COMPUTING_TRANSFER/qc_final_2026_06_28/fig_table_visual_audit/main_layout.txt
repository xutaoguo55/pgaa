11   Abstract
12   Single-cell perturbation datasets often contain subset-confined responses rather than uniform mean shifts,
13   making target prioritization diﬀicult for average-based analyses. We present PGAA, a distribution-aware
14   computational framework for ranking heterogeneous transcriptional responses in single-cell perturbation
15   data. PGAA combines PGAA-W Wasserstein, a 99-point quantile-grid statistic for full-expression-
16   distribution shifts, with the PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic, a secondary diagnostic for responder-
17   associated expression-shape changes. Both statistics use permutation calibration and Storey upper-tail
18   diagnostics. In an Adamson 2016 unfolded-protein-response CRISPRi proof-of-principle benchmark,
19   PGAA-W Wasserstein achieved mean AUROC 0.786 across five pre-specified perturbations and mean
20   AUPRC 0.0191 against a 0.0065 random baseline. A Norman 2019 multi-perturbation CRISPRa extension
21   supported PGAA-W as the default ranking statistic in curated target-panel checks, while the Norman
22   CEBPE contrast illustrated the narrower use of PGAA-H: ELANE ranked 57/2012 as ranking evidence, not
23   FDR-controlled discovery. A processed-source-data MMD-PSM comparison and observational single-cell
24   datasets are retained as bounded comparator and marker-recovery stress checks. PGAA provides a
25   reproducible ranking layer for hypothesis generation in perturbation and single-cell disease-model analyses,
26   with Python/R software, public code and a supplementary reproducibility archive.
27   Keywords: single-cell RNA-seq, Perturb-seq, computational biology, Wasserstein distance, histogram-shape
28   statistic, distributional ranking, CRISPR screening


29   1. Introduction
30   Single-cell perturbation screens 1–4 provide a scalable way to connect genetic or transcriptional perturba-
31   tions to cellular phenotypes. A central computational task in these datasets is to rank genes or response
32   programs whose expression differs between perturbed and control cells. The standard analysis compares
33   mean expression of each non-target gene between groups. This works well when a perturbation produces a
34   uniform transcriptional shift: knock out a transcription factor, its targets drop, and a t-test, Wilcoxon test,
35   MAST, or SCEPTRE can detect the difference 5,6 . Together with preprocessing workflows in Seurat 7,8 and
36   Scanpy 9 , these methods form the standard Perturb-seq analysis toolkit.
37   Many perturbation responses, however, are not well summarized by a mean shift. CRISPRa 10 can activate a
38   target in only a fraction of cells. A weak perturbation may shift variance, create responder and non-responder
39   subpopulations, or change the shape of an expression distribution while leaving the mean only modestly
40   changed. Similar response structures can also appear in marker-anchored contrasts from patient-derived
41   single-cell data, where disease-associated programs often mark subsets of malignant, immune, stromal, or
42   inflammatory cells rather than an entire population. A method that only asks whether average expression
43   changed can under-rank such signals and can make downstream prioritization less interpretable.
44   Few existing Perturb-seq methods make distribution-shape changes a primary output, although
45   heterogeneity-aware approaches are emerging 11 . This leaves a gap between calibrated mean-shift


                                                           1
46   testing and the practical need to rank heterogeneous responses for follow-up experiments. The gap is
47   computational rather than only biological: users need statistics that compare full expression distributions,
48   calibration diagnostics that reveal when a ranking is trustworthy, and software that can be applied
49   consistently across perturbation screens and marker-anchored single-cell contrasts.
50   We present PGAA, a distribution-aware framework and software implementation for heterogeneous single-
51   cell response prioritization. PGAA uses two complementary statistics sharing a common calibration infras-
52   tructure: the PGAA-W Wasserstein quantile-grid statistic captures one-dimensional distributional shifts,
53   and the PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic ranks expression-shape changes consistent with responder-
54   associated heterogeneity. The mathematical target is to rank non-target genes by perturbation-associated
55   distributional discrepancy after coarse cell-state and library-size adjustment. The novelty is not the in-
56   vention of Wasserstein distance or histogram peak scoring; it is their calibration-aware adaptation into a
57   reproducible single-cell perturbation ranking layer with explicit evidence boundaries. Additional modules for
58   conditional MI and Fisher NB scoring are provided as software extensions. All statistics are accompanied by
59   Storey upper-tail calibration diagnostics that let users assess reliability on their own data before interpreting
60   results. Figure 1a illustrates the response regimes motivating PGAA, and Figure 1b summarizes the analysis
61   workflow.
62   We evaluate PGAA across experimental Perturb-seq benchmarks, simulation ablations, distribution-aware
63   comparators, and external marker-recovery stress checks. Adamson 2016 UPR CRISPRi and Norman 2019
64   CRISPRa provide real perturbation benchmarks with complementary response regimes. The Norman anal-
65   yses include a multi-perturbation PGAA-W extension across KLF1, CEBPE, and CEBPA with KS as an
66   additional distribution-aware baseline, followed by a focused CEBPE PGAA-H example. Simulations define
67   the operating regimes of the Wasserstein and PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostics. A processed-source-
68   data MMD-PSM comparison is included as an additional distribution-aware baseline for the Norman CEBPE
69   contrast. Observational scRNA-seq datasets in chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), sepsis, rheumatoid
70   arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease, and PBMCs are used only to ask whether distributional ranking can
71   recover known marker biology in medically relevant single-cell contexts. These analyses support marker

75   This separation is used throughout the manuscript: perturbation benchmarks assess ranking behavior, ob-
76   servational datasets are used only as marker-recovery stress checks, and calibration analyses define when a
77   score should be interpreted. With these evidence boundaries stated, Figure 1 gives the conceptual overview
78   of the response regimes and the PGAA workflow.
79   The statistics in PGAA are deliberately assigned different roles. PGAA-W Wasserstein measures location,
80   spread and other one-dimensional distributional shifts and is the default score. KS measures the maximum
81   empirical CDF discrepancy and is used as a distribution-aware comparator. MMD-PSM and related energy or
82   kernel methods test broader distributional discrepancies but can be computationally heavier and depend on
83   implementation choices. PGAA-H histogram-shape measures peak-structure changes, including responder-
84   associated expression-shape shifts, and is treated as a secondary diagnostic. Mean-based tests remain useful
85   when the response is dominated by a location shift but can under-rank subset-confined or shape-changing
86   responses.

88    2.1 Framework overview and evidence boundaries

89    PGAA is designed as a modular computational ranking layer for heterogeneous single-cell responses. The
90    Wasserstein statistic is the primary starting score for full-distribution shifts, whereas the PGAA-H histogram-
91    shape diagnostic is a secondary diagnostic for responder-associated expression-shape changes. The framework
92    first adjusts expression for coarse cell-state and library-size effects, then ranks genes by distributional dis-
93    crepancy between perturbed and control cells. Within-cluster permutation is used to provide empirical
94    ranking evidence, and the Storey upper-tail diagnostic is used as a guardrail for calibration rather than as
95    a guarantee of genome-wide discovery.
96    Figure 1 summarizes the response-regime problem and the analysis workflow. The figure intentionally sep-
97    arates the default PGAA-W score from the secondary PGAA-H diagnostic, because the benchmarks below
98    support a hierarchy rather than two interchangeable discovery tests.


99    2.2 Independent Perturb-seq benchmark in unfolded-protein-response biology

100   To test PGAA on a small independent Perturb-seq dataset with a complementary perturbation modality
101   and a disease-relevant stress-response pathway, we applied both statistics to the Adamson et al. (2016) UPR
102   CRISPRi screen (GSE90546). After QC, 5,680 K562 cells were retained across five well-characterized sgRNA
103   perturbations targeting UPR genes (SPI1, ZNF326, BHLHE40, CREB1, DDIT3; 468-686 cells each) and a
104   non-targeting control (1,759 cells). The reference positive set was a curated UPR marker set covering IRE1,
105   PERK, ATF6, ERAD, and chaperone branches, of which 13 markers were present in the 2,000-gene HVG
106   subset (Supplementary Table 1).
107   Across the five perturbations, the Wasserstein statistic achieved a mean AUROC of 0.786 (range 0.767-
108   0.806, mean AUPRC 0.0191) for recovering known UPR genes (Table 2). By comparison, Wilcoxon, t-test,
109   and MAST achieved mean AUROCs of 0.529, 0.523, and 0.406 respectively in the same benchmark. The
110   PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic achieved a mean AUROC of 0.748 (range 0.658-0.833, mean AUPRC
111   0.0253). For the BHLHE40 knockdown, this histogram-shape statistic had the highest AUROC and AUPRC
112   among the compared methods (AUROC 0.833, AUPRC 0.0594 vs. Wasserstein AUROC 0.788), consistent
113   with a heterogeneous responder-associated expression pattern (Supplementary Figure 1). Because only
114   13 of the 2,000 HVGs were UPR positives, the random AUPRC baseline was 0.0065; observed AUPRC
115   values correspond to 2.9x and 3.9x enrichment over random expectation for the Wasserstein and histogram-
116   shape statistics, respectively. These perturbations were selected a priori based on UPR annotation in the
117   original study and a minimum cell-count threshold of at least 400 cells, not based on PGAA performance
118   (Supplementary Table 2). The Adamson benchmark supports proof-of-principle prioritization in a small
119   curated UPR setting, but it does not establish general performance across perturbation modalities, cell
120   types, or disease models.

122   mark design, average AUROC, AUPRC relative to the random positive-rate baseline, and perturbation-level

128   2.3 A Norman multi-perturbation extension supports PGAA-W as the default ranking statistic

129   To broaden the real Perturb-seq evaluation beyond the small Adamson UPR benchmark, we added a Norman
130   2019 multi-perturbation CRISPRa extension covering KLF1, CEBPE, and CEBPA. Each perturbation was
131   compared with non-targeting controls using 2,000 HVGs plus forced inclusion of the pre-specified target-
132   panel genes. This forced-inclusion step is important: none of the KLF1, CEBPE, or CEBPA positive-panel
133   genes were marked as HVGs in the processed Norman h5ad file, so a strict HVG-only benchmark would have
134   silently removed the evaluation genes. The script reports a panel audit, gene-level scores, and a metadata
135   table in the supplementary software archive.
136   Across the three perturbations, PGAA-W Wasserstein produced higher target-panel AUROC than the KS
137   statistic, Welch t statistic, or Wilcoxon rank-sum score in this curated-panel benchmark (Supplementary
138   Table 3). The effect was not a claim of high-precision discovery: only 1-2 positive genes appeared in the top


                                                           6
139   100 for each PGAA-W run, and AUPRC values must be read against random baselines of 0.0045-0.0079.
140   The result is therefore useful as a broader perturbation-level ranking check for PGAA-W, not as a substitute
141   for genome-scale perturbation validation or original-study DEG benchmarking.


142   2.4 The PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic provides a narrow CEBPE CRISPRa ranking
143   example

144   Norman et al. (2019) used CRISPRa to activate CEBPE in a K562 Perturb-seq screen 3 ; K562 is a human
145   leukemia cell line 12 . CEBPE is a myeloid transcription factor whose known targets include nine neutrophil
146   granule proteins: ELANE, AZU1, MPO, LYZ, CTSG, GFI1, PRTN3, DEFA1, and RNASE2 13,14 . This
147   benchmark is not a clinical disease cohort, but it is relevant to hematologic differentiation biology and tests
148   the response regime that motivated PGAA: a regulator is activated, but the downstream expression response
149   may be heterogeneous across cells.
150   SCEPTRE and the Wasserstein statistic rank this target poorly. SCEPTRE places ELANE at rank
151   1761/2012 (p = 0.92). The Wasserstein statistic places it at rank 1452/2012 (p = 0.223). Neither method
152   ranks ELANE among its strongest CEBPE candidates in this analysis. As an additional distribution-aware
153   comparator, an MMD-PSM analysis on the same processed CEBPE contrast achieved target-set AUROC
154   0.499 and 0/9 nominal CEBPE target hits. This comparator is useful because it tests a distributional null,
155   but it did not prioritize the curated CEBPE target program in this processed-source-data benchmark.
156   The PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic (n_bins = 20, 500 permutations) ranks ELANE at position 57
157   (p = 0.04), a 25-fold rank improvement over the Wasserstein statistic (Table 3). With 500 permutations,
158   this p-value is interpreted as ranking evidence rather than genome-wide significance, and no FDR-controlled
159   CEBPE gene discovery is claimed. The p-value distribution is acceptable in this pre-specified setting (un-
160   capped Storey upper-tail ratio = 1.32 with 200 permutations in the sensitivity run; approximately 1.0 with
161   500 permutations). The top genes by raw PGAA-H value (SLC45A1, DLX2, ATP5E) are not known CEBPE
162   targets; ELANE’s stronger rank by permutation p-value reflects its favorable null distribution relative to
163   other genes. Across the full nine-gene CEBPE target set, only ELANE reaches p < 0.05 in the PGAA-H
164   analysis; PRTN3 is also within the top 100 by permutation p-value but does not pass the nominal threshold
165   (p = 0.068). The AUROC is 0.476 and AUPRC is 0.0076. Thus, the Norman result should be read as a
166   focused ELANE/PRTN3 ranking signal, not broad recovery of the complete granulocytic target program.
167   As a calibration check, we applied the PGAA-H diagnostic to all six perturbations using the CEBPE target
168   gene set as the reference positive set. ELANE was ranked strongly for CEBPE and also for the severely
169   over-sensitive BAK1 perturbation, where the tail ratio was 0.10. For the well-calibrated KLF1 perturba-
170   tion, ELANE was not supported (p = 0.70, uncapped tail ratio = 1.15). For CBL, SLC4A1, and DUSP9,
171   ELANE p-values were 0.58, 0.16, and 0.90 respectively. This analysis is treated as perturbation-level calibra-
172   tion context rather than specificity proof; over-sensitive perturbations should not be interpreted as formal
173   discoveries. Target-level details are provided in Supplementary Table 4.

181   2.5 Perturbation-specific calibration defines guardrails for PGAA-H ranking

182   Applying the PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic to six different perturbations from the Norman 2019
183   screen reveals that calibration is far from consistent. The uncapped Storey upper-tail ratio ranges from


                                                           8
184   1.15 for KLF1, a well-calibrated erythroid transcription-factor perturbation, to 0.10 for BAK1, a severely
185   over-sensitive perturbation. Intermediate cases include CBL (tail ratio = 0.72), SLC4A1 (0.67), DUSP9
186   (0.68), and CEBPE in the n_bins = 50 calibration run (0.25). Figure 4 shows the full calibration pattern.
187   KLF1 drives a clean erythroid program. CEBPE CRISPRa and BAK1 do not. The calibration spread
188   reflects this biology, and the practical takeaway is simple: include a negative control perturbation and
189   check the upper-tail calibration diagnostic before interpreting PGAA-H ranks. Importantly, the CEBPE tail
190   ratio of 0.25 reported here comes from a calibration run with n_bins = 50 and n_perms = 200; the main
191   analysis in Section 2.4 uses n_bins = 20 and n_perms = 500, which yields an upper-tail ratio approximately
192   1.0-1.3 before capping. The corresponding QQ plot and permutation p-value histogram are provided in
193   Supplementary Figure 3. The discrepancy illustrates how strongly calibration depends on both bin count
194   and permutation depth, reinforcing the need for the pilot sweep recommended in Section 2.6.

195   2.6 Sensitivity to histogram bin count

196   The PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic depends on the number of histogram bins, and the dependence
197   is non-trivial. We tested n_bins values of 20, 30, 50, 75, 100 and 150 on Norman 2019 CEBPE, using
198   200 permutations for the sensitivity sweep; the main CEBPE result uses 500 permutations. The full sweep
199   is reported in Supplementary Table 5. In brief, ELANE ranks well at n_bins = 20 (rank 32, p = 0.025,
200   uncapped tail ratio = 1.32 in the 200-permutation sweep), collapses at n_bins = 30 (rank 1807, p=0.672),
201   and larger bin counts produce many nominally significant genes with low tail-ratio values (0.23-0.25). This is
202   a sharp failure mode rather than a gradual degradation. We therefore treat PGAA-H as a guarded diagnostic
203   with a pre-specified decision rule: choose n_bins from a small pilot grid before interpreting the perturbation
204   of interest, require an acceptable upper-tail diagnostic on a negative-control or unrelated perturbation, and
205   do not tune n_bins to improve a target-gene rank. We recommend n_bins = 20 as the manuscript starting
206   setting, with a pilot sweep over n_bins = 10, 20, 30 and 50 on a control or null perturbation to verify that
207   the chosen value does not fall into a peak-merging failure mode.


208   2.7 Simulation ablation maps each statistic to its regime

209   To systematically assess when each statistic performs best, we simulated three perturbation types at seven
210   effect sizes from theta = 0.1 to theta = 1.0, with three replicates each (63 conditions total). These simulations
211   are intended as operating-regime diagnostics rather than a full generative model of scRNA-seq count data.
212   Type A (pure mean shift) is the Wasserstein statistic’s home territory (TPR = 0.97 at theta = 1.0). Type
213   C (mean + bimodality) favors the Wasserstein statistic as well. The most informative condition is Type B,
214   where only 40% of perturbed cells respond. Here the Wasserstein statistic and the exploratory combined z-
215   score are competitive (TPR = 0.52-0.85 depending on theta), while PGAA-H reaches only TPR = 0.12. This


                                                             9
216   simulation uses partial bimodality–the two modes overlap in the 40%-response regime. The Norman 2019
217   CEBPE analysis therefore should not be read as broad evidence that PGAA-H prioritizes all heterogeneous
218   responses; rather, it illustrates a narrower ranking use case in which a responder-associated expression pattern
219   can improve the rank of selected targets.
220   These results support a simple analysis hierarchy. The Wasserstein statistic is the starting ranking score for
221   standard knockout or overexpression screens and for unknown response modes. The PGAA-H histogram-
222   shape diagnostic should be used only as a secondary diagnostic when discrete responder states are biologically
223   plausible, and only after negative-control calibration and bin-sensitivity checks. The combined z-score can
224   be used as an exploratory ranking score when both mean/distributional shift and shape change are expected,
225   but it is not treated as an inferential discovery test here.
226   The full simulation plot is provided in Supplementary Figure 4.


227   2.8 Observational marker-recovery stress checks remain secondary evidence

228   As an external stress check outside curated perturbation benchmarks, Wasserstein enriches known pathway
229   markers 2.1-5.8x over background across five observational single-cell datasets with clinical or disease rele-
230   vance (CLL 4.0x, Sepsis 2.1x, RA 2.5x, PBMC 2.9x, IBD 5.8x). On CLL, the B-cell receptor signaling gene
231   set (CD79A, CD79B, MS4A1, CD24, BANK1, LYN, BLNK, SYK, BTK, PLCG2, PIK3CD, CD19, CD22)
232   appears prominently in the top 100, with AUROC 0.87 for known-marker recovery (Supplementary Table 6).
233   This result is biologically coherent because B-cell receptor signaling is a central axis in CLL, but the analysis
234   should be read as marker prioritization from public observational data rather than as causal or biomarker
235   evidence.
236   These five datasets are observational–the “perturbation” is defined by binning cells on endogenous marker
237   or pathway-associated expression, not by an experimental intervention–so the results demonstrate marker
238   recovery rather than causal interpretation. In the observational CLL marker-recovery setting, Wasserstein
239   produced biologically coherent rankings but was not uniformly superior to all conventional ranking baselines.
240   Its value here is as a distribution-aware prioritization score for heterogeneous disease-state contrasts, not as
241   a replacement for standard marker-ranking methods.
242   The CLL TCL1A case shows how the two distributional statistics can prioritize different marker programs.
243   Among the top 100 genes ranked by each statistic, only six overlap. The Wasserstein statistic enriches B-cell
244   receptor markers (CD79A rank 9, CD79B rank 48, MS4A1 rank 53), matching the malignant B-cell biology
245   of CLL. The PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic picks up T-cell receptor genes (TRBV7-6, TRAV12-1,
246   CD3E, CD3D) that Wasserstein misses entirely, consistent with a distinct immune-context signal rather
247   than the same B-cell receptor program. The exploratory combined z-score recovers 3 BCR and 11 TCR
248   genes at nominal p < 0.05, versus 4+3 for Wasserstein alone and 0+7 for PGAA-H alone; these nominal
249   combined scores are used only to illustrate rank complementarity.
250   The CLL analysis uses rank-based inverse-normal scores (Supplementary Methods, CLL rank-score analysis)
251   rather than full permutation calibration–a lighter-weight approach appropriate when only relative gene
252   ordering matters rather than formal significance testing (Supplementary Figure 5). The cross-dataset marker-
253   recovery source data and CLL comparator results are retained in Supplementary Table 6. We do not use
254   these observational datasets as causal or clinical evidence. Figure 5 summarizes the external marker-recovery
255   stress checks and the CLL comparator analysis.

256   3. Discussion
257   We set out to assess whether distribution-aware statistics could improve computational prioritization of
258   single-cell perturbation programs when the signal is heterogeneous rather than a simple mean shift. The
259   answer, based on Perturb-seq benchmarks, simulation ablations, distribution-aware comparators, and obser-
260   vational marker-recovery stress checks, is cautiously positive. On the small Adamson 2016 UPR CRISPRi
261   benchmark, the Wasserstein statistic achieved a mean AUROC of 0.786 across five perturbations, higher than
262   Wilcoxon (0.529), t-test (0.523), and MAST (0.406) in this benchmark. Because the positive set contains
263   only 13 genes among 2,000 HVGs, AUPRC values must be interpreted against the 0.0065 random baseline
264   rather than as large absolute precision values. In the Norman 2019 multi-perturbation extension, PGAA-
265   W Wasserstein had higher target-panel AUROC than KS, Welch t, and Wilcoxon for KLF1, CEBPE, and
266   CEBPA, but top-100 positive recovery remained sparse. In the BHLHE40 knockdown case, the PGAA-H
267   histogram-shape diagnostic had the highest AUROC among the compared methods (0.833), consistent with
268   a heterogeneous responder-associated expression pattern. On Norman 2019 CEBPE CRISPRa, PGAA-H
269   ranked the known neutrophil granule target ELANE at position 57, while SCEPTRE and the Wasserstein
270   statistic placed it at 1761 and 1452; across the full nine-gene target set, however, recovery remained limited.
271   The MMD-PSM distribution-aware baseline achieved AUROC 0.499 with 0/9 nominal target hits in the same
272   processed CEBPE comparison. Across five observational datasets used as external stress checks, Wasserstein
273   ranking recovered known marker programs, including B-cell receptor signaling in CLL.
274   These results suggest a practical two-pronged strategy for distribution-aware single-cell perturbation analysis.
275   The Wasserstein statistic is a useful starting distributional score when the perturbation type is unknown,
276   including when marker-anchored scRNA-seq contrasts are used to prioritize candidate response programs for
277   follow-up. The PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic fills a narrower niche–CRISPRa screens, weak-effect
278   perturbations, and any setting where the response is expected to be heterogeneous–but requires the user to
279   verify calibration via the Storey upper-tail diagnostic and to run a pilot n_bins sweep before committing
280   to results. PGAA-H’s strong dependence on histogram bin count (values at n_bins = 20 and n_bins =
281   50 correlate at only r = 0.12) means it should be treated as a ranking tool rather than a formal discovery
282   procedure unless substantially more permutations are used.
283   Several limitations should be noted. First, the Adamson 2016 benchmark is small and has a sparse positive
284   set, so it is proof-of-principle evidence rather than broad validation. Second, the Norman multi-perturbation
285   extension uses curated target-program panels rather than an original-study genome-wide DEG gold stan-
286   dard; its sparse top-rank recovery should be viewed as ranking enrichment, not strong discovery perfor-
287   mance. Third, PGAA-H is calibrated differently for different perturbations; the six-perturbation calibration
288   check showed uncapped upper-tail ratios ranging from 0.10 (BAK1, over-sensitive) to 1.15 (KLF1, well-
289   calibrated), so a negative control perturbation is essential. Fourth, the MI and Fisher NB modules are
290   provided as software extensions but have not been benchmarked. Fifth, our simulation uses Gaussian noise
291   for computational eﬀiciency; we verified that PGAA-H does not spuriously increase under a zero-inflated
292   negative-binomial null (mean PGAA-H = 0.031 with 30% dropout; Supplementary Methods, simulation and
293   zero-inflation controls), consistent with evidence that droplet-based scRNA-seq data are well-modeled by the
294   negative binomial without explicit zero-inflation 15 . Sixth, all primary benchmarks use K-means (k = 5) as
295   a cell-state proxy; sensitivity analyses over k = 3, 5, 10 and no residualization are recommended but not yet
296   completed in this submission package. Seventh, broader genome-scale evaluation against SCEPTRE-family
297   and perturbation-specific methods remains necessary. Finally, the observational disease datasets do not
298   establish new causal disease mechanisms or clinical biomarkers; instead, they show how PGAA can prioritize
299   disease-relevant heterogeneous transcriptional responses for experimental follow-up.
300   PGAA fits into the growing ecosystem of Perturb-seq analysis tools and addresses a complementary computa-
301   tional use case. The pertpy framework 11 provides an end-to-end pipeline into which PGAA’s statistics could
302   be integrated as modular ranking components. Mixscape 16 handles the complementary problem of identi-
303   fying cells with failed perturbations, while scMAGeCK 17 operates at the CRISPR screen level. SCEPTRE
      5
304     and its robust extension 18 set a high standard for calibrated permutation testing of mean shifts; PGAA
305   extends this paradigm to distributional and histogram-shape shifts that may matter when only a subset of
306   cells responds. Recent perturbation-response scoring methods quantify heterogeneous response strength at
307   the single-cell level, whereas PGAA operates at the non-target-gene ranking level after coarse cell-state and


                                                            12
308   library-size adjustment 19 . Large curated resources such as scPerturb make broader cross-dataset bench-
309   marking feasible, but this submission restricts itself to source-data-supported proof-of-principle and curated
310   target-panel checks 20 . Thus, PGAA should be read as a calibrated gene-level distributional ranking compo-
311   nent that can sit alongside perturbation-quality, single-cell response-strength, and formal association-testing
312   methods rather than as a replacement for them.
313   The most immediate priority is broader genome-scale evaluation on scPerturb-scale collections, including
314   Replogle 2020/2022 and additional perturbation screens, where thousands of perturbations would allow a
315   systematic assessment of calibration behavior and the identification of perturbation-level features that pre-
316   dict whether Wasserstein or PGAA-H will prove more informative. The main value of PGAA is not to
317   replace mechanistic validation, but to provide a transparent and reproducible ranking layer for heteroge-
318   neous responses that can be followed up in perturbation screens, disease models, patient-derived systems, or
319   therapeutic perturbation experiments.


320   4. Methods
321   4.1 Setup and residualization

322   We work with expression matrices normalized to 10,000 counts per cell and transformed with log1p. After
323   standard QC and highly-variable gene selection (2,000 genes), each cell i has a perturbation label D_i in
324   {0,1} and covariates Z_i. Before applying any statistic, we remove cell-type and library-size effects by
325   ordinary least-squares residualization:
326   X_tilde = X_res = (I - H_Z) X
327   H_Z = Z (Z^T Z)^+ Z^T
328   where H_Z is the projection matrix for the covariate design matrix Z, X_tilde (also denoted X_res) is the
329   residualized expression matrix, and (Z^T Z)^+ denotes the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse used for numerical
330   stability. The matrix Z contains an intercept, k - 1 K-means cluster dummy variables (k = 5, one reference
331   cluster) 21 , and standardized library size. This step is applied to the PGAA-W and PGAA-H statistics; the
332   MI and Fisher NB exploratory scores operate on the original normalized matrix. The value of k is fixed
333   throughout the benchmark as a coarse cell-state proxy rather than estimated from the data.
334   For a target gene g*, we score each non-target gene g for differences between perturbed and control cells in
335   mean, shape, or dependency structure.


336   4.2 Wasserstein distance

337   We approximate the one-dimensional Wasserstein distance by comparing empirical quantile functions on a
338   fixed 99-point percentile grid. Let Q_hat_{g,d}(u) denote the empirical u-quantile of residualized expression
339   for gene g among cells with perturbation indicator D_i = d. The score is
340   PGAA-W(g) = (1/99) sum_{r=1}^{99} |Q_hat_{g,1}(r/100) - Q_hat_{g,0}(r/100)|
341   Wasserstein distances provide a non-parametric way to compare full one-dimensional distributions and can
342   respond to changes in location, spread, or shape. This makes the statistic useful when the biological signal
343   is not limited to a mean shift. The 1D case has an eﬀicient quantile-based computation 22,23 .
344   We compute p-values via within-cluster permutation 24,25 . For B permutations, we shuffle D within each
345   K-means cluster, recompute the Wasserstein statistic, and use the plus-one estimator
346   p_perm = [1 + sum_{b=1}^{B} I{S_b >= S_obs}] / (B + 1)
347   where S_obs is the observed statistic and S_b is the statistic from permutation b. The cluster-stratified
348   shuffle preserves cell-type composition while randomizing the perturbation assignment.
349   A note on numerical consistency. The observed and permuted W1 statistics are computed with the same
350   99-quantile approximation. This is essential because permutation p-values compare the observed statistic


                                                            13
351   directly with its empirical null distribution. We verified calibration empirically: under the null, the observed
352   false positive rate is 0.057 (30 simulations, 95% CI [0.021, 0.079]).


353   4.3 PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic

354   The PGAA-H histogram-shape diagnostic summarizes peak-structure changes in the one-dimensional expres-
355   sion histogram, such as the emergence or disappearance of peaks. For each gene g, we compute histograms
356   of X_tilde[, g] for perturbed cells (D_i = 1) and control cells (D_i = 0) using gene-specific shared bin edges.
357   The manuscript starting setting is n_bins = 20 equally spaced bins over that gene’s pooled perturbed-control
358   expression range. We then identify local maxima and assign each peak a death level defined by the higher of
359   the adjacent saddle levels, using the nearest higher peak or boundary on each side. This is a one-dimensional
360   peak-prominence construction derived from the Elder Rule used in topological data analysis 26–29 . The peak-
361   prominence value is the peak height minus this death level. For each condition, we keep the three largest
362   peak-prominence values and zero-pad when fewer than three peaks are present.
363   The PGAA-H diagnostic is the root-mean-square distance between the top-3 peak-prominence vectors:
364   PGAA-H(g) = sqrt((1/3) * sum_{r=1}^{3} (v_{g,1,r} - v_{g,0,r})^2)
365   Here v_{g,d,r} is the r-th largest peak-prominence value for gene g in condition d, where d = 1 denotes
366   perturbed cells and d = 0 denotes control cells. This plain-text notation is used to keep the formula stable
367   in DOCX and PDF conversion.
368   When a CRISPRa perturbation activates a gene in only a fraction of cells, the expression histogram can
369   change from a single dominant pattern to a heterogeneous responder-associated pattern. The dominant peak-
370   prominence value may then increase substantially, yielding large PGAA-H values. A pure mean shift that
371   preserves the histogram shape leaves PGAA-H comparatively unchanged. The contrast between these two
372   scenarios is what makes PGAA-H complementary to the Wasserstein statistic, but PGAA-H is interpreted
373   here as a ranking and diagnostic statistic rather than as a standalone genome-wide discovery test.
374   The choice of n_bins matters. Our sensitivity analysis (Section 2.6) tests n_bins values of 20, 30, 50, 75, 100,
375   and 150. The manuscript starting setting, n_bins = 20, is acceptably calibrated in the Norman 2019 CEBPE
376   run (uncapped upper-tail ratio approximately 1.0-1.3 depending on permutation depth). Larger values can
377   produce more nominal hits at the cost of over-sensitive calibration. For new analyses, we recommend a
378   guarded PGAA-H workflow: pre-specify a small n_bins grid such as 10, 20, 30 and 50; evaluate the grid
379   on a negative-control, null, or unrelated perturbation; keep a setting only if the upper-tail diagnostic is
380   acceptable and the p-value histogram is not over-sensitive; and then apply the chosen setting unchanged to
381   the perturbation of interest. We use 500 within-cluster permutations for the null. With 500 permutations, the
382   minimum attainable p-value is 1/501, approximately 0.0020, which is above the Bonferroni threshold for 2,000
383   genes (2.5e-5). The PGAA-H permutation p-values therefore support ranking-based evaluation (AUROC)
384   rather than formal FDR-controlled discovery; for genome-wide significance, we recommend n_perms >=
385   40,000.


386   4.4 Additional modules

387   Two additional scoring modules are included in the software package as extensions. The conditional MI
388   module explores whether perturbation alters gene-gene dependency structure via k-NN entropy estimators
      30
389      . The Fisher NB module explores perturbation-induced shifts using the Fisher information metric on the
390   negative binomial family. These modules are not benchmarked or claimed as primary contributions; they
391   are provided for users who wish to explore dependency or count-model effects. Algorithm details are given
392   in the Supplementary Methods.


393   4.5 Combined exploratory z-score

394   Under independence and correct calibration, the combined exploratory z-score is


                                                            14
395   z_comb = (1/sqrt(k)) sum_{j=1}^{k} Phi^{-1}(1 - p_j).
396   This score is asymptotically standard normal under H0. We use k = 2 (Wasserstein + PGAA-H) as an
397   exploratory rank-combination score. Because both statistics are computed from the same expression dis-
398   tributions, the independence assumption is unlikely to hold exactly; formal combined-score inference would
399   require an empirical covariance adjustment, Brown’s method, or a permutation-calibrated combination.
400   In this manuscript, the combined score is used only for exploratory ranking. For Norman 2019 CEBPE,
401   PGAA-H alone ranked ELANE higher than the combination.


402   4.6 Calibration diagnostics

403   For calibration, PGAA reports the Storey upper-tail diagnostic 31,32 . Let p_i be the permutation p-value
404   for ranked gene i, let m be the number of ranked genes, and set lambda = 0.5. The uncapped upper-tail
405   calibration ratio is
406   R_lambda = #{i: p_i > lambda} / [(1 - lambda) * m].
407   The capped Storey null-fraction estimate is pi0_hat(lambda) = min(1, R_lambda). The uncapped ratio
408   R_lambda is reported separately as a calibration diagnostic: values near or above 1 indicate no obvious
409   excess of small p-values, whereas values far below 1 indicate over-sensitivity or anti-conservative behavior.
410   This wording avoids the mathematically incorrect implication that the null fraction pi0 itself can exceed 1.
411   We also recommend running the PGAA-H diagnostic on an unrelated target gene from the same screen (e.g.,
412   KLF1 from Norman 2019) as a negative perturbation control, and verifying that the calibration diagnostic
413   remains acceptable.


414   4.7 Datasets

415   We use seven publicly available datasets: five observational scRNA-seq datasets for marker-recovery stress
416   checks and two Perturb-seq datasets for method benchmarking. The observational analyses include CLL
417   (GSE111014; 36,601 cells, subsampled to 20,000; TCL1A high/low grouping with B-cell receptor mark-
418   ers), sepsis (GSE167363; 64,244 cells, subsampled to 20,000; TCR pathway markers), rheumatoid arthri-
419   tis (GSE159117; 10,499 cells; cytokine pathway markers), the 10x Genomics 3k demo PBMC dataset 33
420   (2,700 cells; multi-lineage markers), and inflammatory bowel disease (GSE116222; 11,175 cells; gut epithelial
421   disease-associated markers).
422   The perturbation benchmarks are Norman 2019 (GSE133344) and Adamson 2016 (GSE90546). For Norman
423   2019, we use the K562 CRISPRa screen for the CEBPE ranking example, the six-perturbation PGAA-H
424   calibration check, and the multi-perturbation PGAA-W extension across KLF1, CEBPE, and CEBPA. The
425   six PGAA-H calibration perturbations are CEBPE, KLF1, SLC4A1, BAK1, DUSP9, and CBL; the CEBPE
426   target set is ELANE, AZU1, MPO, LYZ, CTSG, GFI1, PRTN3, DEFA1, and RNASE2. The Norman multi-
427   perturbation extension uses curated target-program panels for KLF1, CEBPE, and CEBPA, 2,000 HVGs plus
428   forced inclusion of the panel genes, and non-targeting controls sampled at up to a 3:1 control-to-perturbed
429   ratio. The comparator scores in that extension are PGAA-W Wasserstein, two-sample KS statistic, absolute
430   Welch t statistic, and Wilcoxon -log10(p). For Adamson 2016, we analyze five pre-specified UPR-associated
431   CRISPRi perturbations (SPI1, ZNF326, BHLHE40, CREB1, and DDIT3) against non-targeting controls,
432   using the curated UPR marker set in Supplementary Table 1.
433   All datasets are normalized to 10,000 counts per cell and log-transformed after quality control. Analyses use
434   2,000 highly variable genes, with forced inclusion of target genes where needed.

438   package includes Python and R smoke/regression checks for the core Wasserstein and PGAA-H calculations.
